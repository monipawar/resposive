Deceased Donation

Organs for transplantation can come from living doner and non-living doner both.brain dead people who, with their previous consent or the consent of their families, have chosen the selfless choice to donate their organs. Unfortunately, there are not nearly enough deceased donor organs available to perform transplants on everyone. The transplant community has worked hard in recent years to use every organ available from every donor. Not every given kidney is ideal, but transplant physicians have long pondered if kidneys that weren't previously considered could be effectively used if they were paired with the correct recipients.

Types of living organs

  • Heart
  • Liver
  • Two Lungs
  • Pancreas
  • Two kidneys
  • Heart

The heart can be transplanted to replace a failing or diseased heart in a recipient. Heart transplantation is typically reserved for severe cases of heart failure.

  • Liver

Liver transplantation involves replacing a damaged or diseased liver with a healthy liver from a deceased donor. It is a common treatment for end-stage liver disease or acute liver failure.

  • Two lungs

Lung transplantation is performed to replace damaged or diseased lungs, often due to conditions such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), cystic fibrosis, pulmonary fibrosis, or other severe lung disorders.

  • Pancreas

Pancreas transplantation is typically performed in conjunction with a kidney transplant in recipients with diabetes and severe kidney disease. It can help restore normal insulin production.

  • Two kidneys

Kidney transplantation is a common and highly successful procedure where one or both kidneys from a deceased donor are transplanted into a recipient with end-stage renal disease (ESRD) or kidney failure.
